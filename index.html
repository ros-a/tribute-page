Walter Gropius

In 1919 Walter Gropius founded the art and design school Staatliches Bauhaus, more commonly known as Bauhaus. Rejecting the elitist and conservatist schools of the past, Bauhaus aimed to break down the barriers between art and craftmanship. Although the school only lasted 14 years, its short existence did not limit its impact. By the time the Nazi regime closed down the school in 1933, Bauhaus had grown into something much bigger: a new way of thinking had emerged, celebrating interdisciplinarity, diversity and experimentation over all else.

A timeline of Gropius' life

1883

Walter Gropius is born in Berlin to Walter Adolph Gropius, a government official, and Manon Auguste Pauline Scharnweber, daughter of a Prussian politician.

1903

Gropius enrolls at the Technical School in Munich to study Architecture, and later continues studying in Berlin. He never receives a degree.

1908

Gropius joins the office of the renowned architect and industrial designer Peter Behrens. Fellow employees include Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, Le Corbusier, and Dietrich Marcks.

1910

Gropius leaves the firm of Behrens and together with fellow employee Adolf Meyer starts a practice in Berlin.

1913

The build of the Faguswerk, a shoe last factory in Alfeld on the Leine, is finished. This building, designed by Gropius and Meyer, is still today regarded as one of the founding monuments of modernism.

1914

Gropius is drafted and served as a sergeant major at the Western front during the war years, where he was severely wounded and almost got killed.

1919

Gropius founds the Staatliches Bauhaus in Weimar, Germany, as a mergent of the Grand-Ducal Saxon Academy of Fine Art and the Grand Ducal Saxon School of Arts and Crafts.

1925

Bauhaus moves to Dessau, Germany, into a building designed by Gropius. In this new location, Bauhaus would celebrate its heydays.

1928

Gropius resigns and Hannes Meyer takes over as the new Bauhaus director.

1934

With the help of the English architect Maxwell Fry, Gropius manages to leave Nazi Germany and moves to England. The Bauhaus had closed one year earlier, under pressure of the Nazi regime.

1937

Gropius moves to the United States. The house that he built for himself in Lincoln, Massachusetts, was very influential in bringing International Modernism to the U.S.

1945

The Architects' Collaborative (TAC), an American architectural firm, is formed by Walter Gropius and seven younger architects in 1945 in Cambridge, Massachusetts.

1969

Gropius dies in Boston, Massachusetts, aged 86.
